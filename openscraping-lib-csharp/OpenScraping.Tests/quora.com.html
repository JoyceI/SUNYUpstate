From my personal experience http://www.kickstarter.com/proje... doing research before and after my project and being involved with friends projects I'd say there are absolutely things you can do to make your project more successful and stick out:

  1. Make a video. Lots of people don't.
  2. Make a good video. Even if it's just you sitting there do it well. If you're asking for a lot of money consider spending some on your video. Have someone clever edit it, or direct it. There are a lot of examples of great easy videos and you can find them yourself just browsing. I'm in this one http://www.kickstarter.com/proje... and we shot it in a short evening. My video for Huck Finn was shot in an hour. Granted both of these were made by film professionals, but they are still very simple.
  3. Do your research. Look at other projects.
  4. Things that are preorders seem to work best. Make your incentives fun and desirable. Make sure you have stuff at lots of levels, especially low ones. Our highest backer level was $25 which was essentially a preorder for the book. We also had a lot of lower backers. If your project doesn't end in something tangible then look to see what kinds of stuff similar successful projects have offered.
  5. In terms of social media you have to try to make your title easily tweet-able and have an interesting picture come up on Facebook. This helped me a lot. People wanted to click on "Replacing the N-word with Robot in Huck Finn" just to see what the hell was going on. We were shared over 14,000 times on Facebook because of that and the cute picture. It needs to be something that catches people's interest. This is harder for some projects, but it's something to aim for.
  6. Promote yourself. If you don't nobody else will. You can't expect to put a project out there and just let it ride. It's a lot of work and might not be right for everybody. Ask people to retweet and share. Some people don't think of it if you don't ask them.
  7. Consider if Kickstarter is right for you. I obviously love Kickstarter. It worked great for me and I hope to use it again. But there are other similar companies out there with different models. The all or nothing money plan for Kickstarter can be scary. If you don't think you'll make your goal and you plan on doing the project regardless consider one of the websites that let you keep whatever you collect.
  8. Consider your goal carefully. We made our goal in 24 hours and the amount of money we raised changed the scope of the project vastly. It looks like mostly the projects that raise much more than their goal seem to be the preorder model. In other projects the successful ones tend to go just a bit above their goal. People think "yay they made it" and move on. You don't want it to be too high but you also don't want it to be too low.
  9. Read back through the Kickstarter blog. They are super helpful and have a lot of metrics and advice there as well as their favorite projects.

We just finished up a campaign about a month ago (http://www.kickstarter.com/proje...) and there were 3 things I think really helped us:

1) Concentrating on the Video's "Story":  Because our video watched more like a story than an informational video, people were more likely to share.We got around 70K views.  I can't state how much the video matters - definitely spend your time on this and don't rush it.

2) Early Traction is Key:  Before launching, we lined up 150 blogs we would reach out to, and emailed them within the first 48 hours.  We also had a "launch party" where we reached out to all our friends and family and asked them to share the project.  Making it easy for friends and family to share (by giving them template Tweets, etc.) allowed us to get early traction, which helped us immensely later on (e.g., TechCrunch wouldn't have found out about us if we didn't get that early traction).  How you get early traction differs from project to project.  While blogs worked great for us (people who were one click away from buying), another company I spoke to reached out hundreds of people individually via Linkedin who they thought would be interested in the project. 

3) Be Ready for A Lot of Hard Work:  We basically worked on Kickstarter from 9 - 5 for 5 days a week.  During the campaign, we got over 2,000 messages and emails which we tried to reply to within 12 hours.  We also constantly updated the project, be that through updating the video, responding to comments, adding prizes, adding stretch goals, or writing backer updates.  Definitely set aside time to do it right and know that it will be a full time job!

After studying a countless number of campaigns, cross referencing data, testing, validating, rinsing, and repeating, there are 3 key things that will have the biggest impact on success. Below is a summary, you can read the full post here.

1. Solve a problem.
Validate that your solution is what people want. Articulate it well, and this becomes your story. Your goal is to communicate to people why your idea needs to exist. If you solve a problem, people will want to help you solve it. Your story is the single most important thing. You must have a video that tells that story, but don't focus on making it look pretty or cool, unless your value proposition is aesthetic value. There are tons of projects that have done amazing well without spending huge marketing dollars on polish.

2. Have a marketing plan.
It's going to be ALOT of hard work. Debunk the myth that 'if you build it they will come', because they won't unless you work for it. Have everything lined up before you launch, marketing, press, social networks, email list, early adopters, events, etc. So that when you actually do, it's all about hitting the send button.

3. Get Early Traction.
80% of the projects that reach 20% of their funding goals are successful. If you ignore this, your project will have trouble getting off the ground, the effect of which only compounds as the countdown timer ticks.

There is a significant amount of planning and preparation that goes into successful Kickstarter projects. Check out my blog post: Bohemian Guitars: Succeeding on Kickstarter about my experiences on Kickstarter: Bohemian Guitars - Oil Can Guitar. I outline some key tools and tips that helped me prepare for our project and manage our project both during and post campaign. Below are my top 9 tips/pieces of advice:

Have an easy to understand call to action – the story or scope of your project needs to be clear and concise; intriguing and compelling. If you have a difficult time communicating what you are trying to accomplish or how you will use the money raised then how is your audience meant to make sense of it? Knowing your own story is the easy part, communicating it effectively to a mass audience requires preparation and planning.
A well-produced video is critical – you’re setting yourself up for failure if you decide not to have a video. If you don’t have a video the odds of someone reading your entire page are slim to none. If you can devote some money to put together a 1 – 2 minute video outlining your story and call to action, do it. It will be well worth the investment.
Mobilize your base – not everyone knows what Kickstarter or crowdfunding is. In fact, I would recommend assuming no one does and at some point whoever you speak to will need some educating on the subject. Maybe Atlanta is slow to adopt to the concept, but we found this to be one of our biggest challenges. I recommend reaching out to friends and family well in advance of the campaign launch and begin educating them early. After all, the first three days of every campaign are the most critical and if you can’t depend on the people you know to provide that initial momentum boost it’s going to be a long campaign.
Have a strong team - I cannot stress this enough. Running a Kickstarter campaign is grueling and it’s almost impossible to do on your own. It’s a 24/7 job and having a team with defined roles that you can depend on is invaluable. It’s important that someone is accessible at all times whether it is responding to questions from potential backers, managing social media, or marketing efforts, someone needs to be available!
Simple rewards and clearly defined stretch goals – don’t over complicate your rewards. I don’t think the number of the reward levels is as significant as the actual rewards. But keep the rewards simple, make it clear what the backer will get in return for his or her donation. Offer exclusive to Kickstarter rewards with limited quantities. Make your backers feel appreciated.Stretch goals are a little trickier. We hit our funding goal in just over 7 days and quickly realized we hadn't adequately defined our stretch goals. We were so excited that we had surpassed our goal so early in the campaign that we rushed out the stretch goal communication. Make sure your stretch goals are as easy to understand as your initial call to action. Stretch goals are important and by offering backers an extra incentive you encourage them to spread the word about your campaign so they themselves can reap the benefits.
Social media engagement – begin growing your social media audience as early as possible. Engage and communicate with your followers both prior to and during the campaign. The more you make them feel like they are part of your ‘community’ the more likely they are to help socialize your campaign. We found that the functionality's from a product like Insightpoo lproved invaluable in helping us identify our relevant audience.
Marketing – marketing is key. Develop a plan for the duration of the campaign and stick to it. Do whatever you can to avoid the lull that occurs in the middle of most projects. We failed in this regard and often tried to scrap together guerrilla marketing schemes at the last minute to generate some more buzz.
Reach out to bloggers – hiring someone to handle PR for your campaign is a must, but you can’t just rely on press releases. Establishing relationships and channels of communications with bloggers and media outlets could prove critical to the overall success of your campaign. For example prior to our go live I was able to get in touch with a writer for TechCrunch and provided him with a sneak preview of our Kickstarter. As a result an article was posted the day we launched which resulted in over 350 tweets and significant traffic to our page.
Reward Management and Fulfillment – before launching your project you need to have a solid grasp of how you are going to fulfill your rewards. In layman’s terms, build relationships with your suppliers, identify all of your costs, determine how quickly you can turnaround each reward, and have a plan in place to distribute your rewards (if you get funded, knock on wood). Whether you have 100 backers or 3,000 backers you’re likely going to run into logistics and fulfillment issues, that’s how life is. But the work you put forth before your campaign will help mitigate the severity of any reward fulfillment issues you may run into along the way. For pledge management I highly recommend checking out leveraging the services of Backerkit. They’re currently helping us stay organized. They've got an excellent system in place that offers our backers transparency and will keep them happy during the reward fulfillment process.

Hard to add much that hasn't been covered but a few points:

PRE-SELL:
-- You want pent up demand for your project the minute it launches. There are stats such as a 30 day project should try and raise 20% of its goal in the first 48 hours. If it does, the chances for success increase greatly. A part of that is the psychology. People want to back a project that will succeed, not waste their time on one that won't. So make them feel really quick like it's going to happen.
-- Use a site like Prefundia.com - "coming soon" with details. Interested potential backers give their email address and are notified the instant your Kickstarter campaign launches.
-- Hit your friends and family hard but in a non-spammy way (if that's possible).

KICKSTARTER PAGE:
-- In addition to what everyone's said, focus on your primary target audience and create the page, the video, the rewards, everything primarily for them first. Make sure it works for them.
-- It's NOT a sales page or a website. It's about telling a story in an engaging way that pulls in potential backers. Engage and excite.
-- Create a mix of text and images that makes someone feel like they're holding your product (if it's a product) in their hands.
-- Spend a few bucks to have great visuals/imagery. People want to back a project they think will succeed. The more professional your page looks, the more credible and the more likely they'll back you.

PROMOTION:
-- Run like crazy with a plan.
-- Look for key backer sites that might be a fit...like BackerClub or BackersHub. (Those two were strongly recommended to me.)
-- Don't assume that you'll get any "staff recommendations" or "trending" or anything else in the Kickstarter communications. If it happens, gravy.
